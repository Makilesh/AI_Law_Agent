# CONSTITUTION OF INDIA - FUNDAMENTAL RIGHTS
# Part III: Articles 12-35
# The Basic Rights Guaranteed to All Citizens

## Article 14 - Equality Before Law

**Article Number:** Article 14
**Part:** Part III - Fundamental Rights
**Type:** Right to Equality

**Text:**
"The State shall not deny to any person equality before the law or the equal protection of the laws within the territory of India."

**Meaning:**
This article guarantees two rights:
1. **Equality before law** - No person is above the law
2. **Equal protection of laws** - Law must treat similar people similarly

**Key Principles:**
- Rule of law
- No arbitrary discrimination
- Similar treatment in similar circumstances
- Reasonable classification permitted

**Exceptions (Reasonable Classification):**
Classification is valid if:
1. Based on intelligible differentia (clear distinction)
2. Differentia has rational relation to object sought

**Examples of Application:**
- Progressive taxation (different tax slabs)
- Reservation for SC/ST/OBC
- Special laws for women and children
- Different retirement ages for different services

**Landmark Cases:**
- State of West Bengal v. Anwar Ali Sarkar (1952)
- E.P. Royappa v. State of Tamil Nadu (1974)
- Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India (1978)

**Scope:**
- Applies to all persons (citizens and non-citizens)
- Applies to all laws (statutory, customary, judicial)
- Negative obligation on State
- Can be enforced against State only, not private parties

## Article 15 - Prohibition of Discrimination

**Article Number:** Article 15
**Part:** Part III - Fundamental Rights
**Type:** Right to Equality

**Text:**
"The State shall not discriminate against any citizen on grounds only of religion, race, caste, sex, place of birth or any of them."

**Prohibited Grounds of Discrimination:**
1. Religion
2. Race
3. Caste
4. Sex
5. Place of birth

**Scope:**
- Applies to citizens only (not non-citizens)
- Prohibition against State discrimination
- Access to shops, public restaurants, hotels, places of public entertainment
- Use of wells, tanks, bathing ghats, roads, places of public resort

**Exceptions (Permitted Discrimination):**
1. **Article 15(3):** Special provisions for women and children
2. **Article 15(4):** Special provisions for socially and educationally backward classes
3. **Article 15(5):** Reservation in educational institutions including private institutions
4. **Article 15(6):** Reservation for economically weaker sections (EWS)

**Important Provisions:**
- 10% reservation for EWS (Annual income below Rs. 8 lakhs)
- Separate facilities for women permitted
- Prohibition of discrimination in admission to educational institutions
- State can make special provisions for advancement of backward classes

**Landmark Cases:**
- Champakam Dorairajan v. State of Madras (1951)
- Indra Sawhney v. Union of India (Mandal Commission case)
- Ashoka Kumar Thakur v. Union of India (OBC reservation case)

## Article 16 - Equality of Opportunity in Public Employment

**Article Number:** Article 16
**Part:** Part III - Fundamental Rights
**Type:** Right to Equality

**Text:**
"There shall be equality of opportunity for all citizens in matters relating to employment or appointment to any office under the State."

**Key Provisions:**

**Article 16(1):** General Rule
- Equality of opportunity in public employment
- Applies to all citizens

**Article 16(2):** Prohibited Discriminations
- No discrimination on grounds of religion, race, caste, sex, descent, place of birth, residence

**Article 16(3):** Residence Requirement
- Parliament can prescribe residence requirement for certain employments

**Article 16(4):** Reservation for Backward Classes
- State can reserve posts for backward classes inadequately represented

**Article 16(4A):** Reservation in Promotions
- Reservation for SC/ST in promotions with consequential seniority

**Article 16(4B):** Carry Forward of Vacancies
- Unfilled reserved vacancies can be carried forward

**Article 16(5):** Religious Institutions
- Law requiring residence or religious affiliation for religious institutions

**Article 16(6):** Economically Weaker Sections
- 10% reservation in direct recruitment for EWS

**Current Reservation Structure:**
- SC: 15%
- ST: 7.5%
- OBC: 27%
- EWS: 10%
- Total: 59.5% (Supreme Court ceiling: 50%, exceptions exist)

**Important Judgments:**
- Indra Sawhney v. Union of India (50% ceiling)
- M. Nagaraj v. Union of India (promotion reservation)
- Janhit Abhiyan v. Union of India (EWS reservation upheld)

## Article 19 - Protection of Freedom of Speech and Expression

**Article Number:** Article 19
**Part:** Part III - Fundamental Rights
**Type:** Right to Freedom

**Article 19(1) - Six Freedoms:**

**(a) Freedom of Speech and Expression**
- Right to express thoughts and opinions
- Includes press freedom
- Right to information
- Right to protest peacefully
- Commercial speech
- Symbolic speech

**(b) Freedom to Assemble Peacefully**
- Right to hold public meetings
- Right to protest
- Must be peaceful and unarmed

**(c) Freedom to Form Associations or Unions**
- Right to form associations
- Right to form trade unions
- Right to form companies
- Right to form political parties

**(d) Freedom to Move Freely**
- Right to move anywhere in India
- Subject to reasonable restrictions

**(e) Freedom to Reside and Settle**
- Right to reside in any part of India
- Right to settle anywhere
- Jammu & Kashmir had special provisions (Article 370 abrogated)

**(g) Freedom to Practice Any Profession**
- Right to practice any profession
- Right to carry on any occupation, trade or business
- Subject to professional qualifications

**Article 19(2)-(6) - Reasonable Restrictions:**

Restrictions must be:
1. **Reasonable** - Not arbitrary
2. **In public interest**
3. **Prescribed by law** - Not executive action

**Grounds for Restrictions:**
- Sovereignty and integrity of India
- Security of State
- Friendly relations with foreign States
- Public order
- Decency or morality
- Contempt of court
- Defamation
- Incitement to offence

**What is NOT Protected:**
- Hate speech
- Obscenity
- Defamation
- Contempt of court
- Incitement to violence
- Speech threatening national security

**Important Cases:**
- Romesh Thappar v. State of Madras (1950)
- Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India (1978)
- S. Rangarajan v. P. Jagjivan Ram (1989)
- Shreya Singhal v. Union of India (2015) - Section 66A IT Act struck down

## Article 20 - Protection Against Ex Post Facto Laws

**Article Number:** Article 20
**Part:** Part III - Fundamental Rights
**Type:** Right Against Exploitation

**Three Protections:**

**Article 20(1) - Ex Post Facto Laws**
"No person shall be convicted of any offence except for violation of a law in force at the time of the commission of the act charged as an offence, nor be subjected to a penalty greater than that which might have been inflicted under the law in force at the time of the commission of the offence."

**Meaning:**
- Cannot be punished for act not illegal when committed
- Cannot be given higher punishment than prescribed when act was committed
- Protects against retrospective criminal laws

**Article 20(2) - Double Jeopardy**
"No person shall be prosecuted and punished for the same offence more than once."

**Conditions for Double Jeopardy:**
1. Same offense
2. Before court of competent jurisdiction
3. Prosecuted and convicted/acquitted
4. Second prosecution for same offense

**Exceptions:**
- Different offenses arising from same facts
- Departmental inquiry + criminal trial (not double jeopardy)
- Appeal/revision (not second prosecution)

**Article 20(3) - Self-Incrimination**
"No person accused of any offence shall be compelled to be a witness against himself."

**Meaning:**
- Right to silence
- Right against self-incrimination
- Cannot be forced to give evidence against oneself
- Applies during investigation, inquiry, and trial

**What is Protected:**
- Oral statements
- Documentary evidence (personal nature)
- Confessions

**What is NOT Protected:**
- Physical evidence (blood sample, DNA, fingerprints)
- Specimen signatures
- Voice samples
- Photographs

**Landmark Cases:**
- State of Bombay v. Kathi Kalu Oghad (1961)
- Nandini Satpathy v. P.L. Dani (1978)
- Selvi v. State of Karnataka (2010) - Narco-analysis

## Article 21 - Protection of Life and Personal Liberty

**Article Number:** Article 21
**Part:** Part III - Fundamental Rights
**Type:** Right to Life and Liberty

**Text:**
"No person shall be deprived of his life or personal liberty except according to procedure established by law."

**Scope (Expanded by Courts):**
This fundamental right includes:

1. **Right to Live with Dignity**
2. **Right to Livelihood**
3. **Right to Health and Medical Care**
4. **Right to Clean Environment**
5. **Right to Food and Water**
6. **Right to Shelter**
7. **Right to Education** (now Article 21A)
8. **Right to Privacy**
9. **Right to Sleep**
10. **Right to Speedy Trial**
11. **Right Against Solitary Confinement**
12. **Right to Legal Aid**
13. **Right Against Custodial Violence**
14. **Right Against Handcuffing**
15. **Right to Compensation for Arrest/Detention**

**"Procedure Established by Law" Must Be:**
1. Right, just, and fair
2. Reasonable
3. Not arbitrary
4. Follows principles of natural justice

**Article 21A - Right to Education:**
- Free and compulsory education for children aged 6-14 years
- Inserted by 86th Amendment, 2002
- Implemented through Right to Education Act, 2009

**Important Judgments:**
- Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India (1978) - Landmark expansion
- Olga Tellis v. Bombay Municipal Corporation (1985) - Right to livelihood
- Francis Coralie Mullin v. UT of Delhi (1981) - Live with dignity
- Vishaka v. State of Rajasthan (1997) - Sexual harassment guidelines
- K.S. Puttaswamy v. Union of India (2017) - Right to privacy

**Article 22 - Protection Against Arrest and Detention:**
Rights of arrested persons:
1. Right to be informed of grounds of arrest
2. Right to consult and defended by lawyer
3. Right to be produced before magistrate within 24 hours
4. Right against detention beyond 24 hours without magistrate's order

## Article 23 - Prohibition of Traffic in Human Beings and Forced Labor

**Article Number:** Article 23
**Part:** Part III - Fundamental Rights
**Type:** Right Against Exploitation

**Text:**
"Traffic in human beings and begar and other similar forms of forced labour are prohibited and any contravention of this provision shall be an offence punishable in accordance with law."

**Three Prohibitions:**

1. **Traffic in Human Beings**
- Buying and selling of humans
- Includes sex trafficking
- Child trafficking
- Bonded labor

2. **Begar (Forced Labor)**
- Compelling someone to work without payment
- Historical practice in feudal India
- Now completely prohibited

3. **Other Forms of Forced Labor**
- Any compulsory service without remuneration
- Exploitation of workers

**Exceptions:**
State can impose compulsory service for public purposes without discrimination:
- Military service
- Social service
- National service
- Emergency service

**Implementing Laws:**
- Bonded Labour System (Abolition) Act, 1976
- Child Labour (Prohibition and Regulation) Act, 1986
- Immoral Traffic (Prevention) Act, 1956
- Transplantation of Human Organs Act, 1994

**Important Cases:**
- Bandhua Mukti Morcha v. Union of India (1984)
- People's Union for Democratic Rights v. Union of India (1982)
- M.C. Mehta v. State of Tamil Nadu (1996) - Child labor in match factories

## Article 24 - Prohibition of Child Labor

**Article Number:** Article 24
**Part:** Part III - Fundamental Rights
**Type:** Right Against Exploitation

**Text:**
"No child below the age of fourteen years shall be employed to work in any factory or mine or engaged in any other hazardous employment."

**Prohibition:**
- Children below 14 years
- Cannot work in factories
- Cannot work in mines
- Cannot work in hazardous industries

**Hazardous Industries:**
- Any work harmful to health, safety, or morals
- Work that interferes with education
- Work in dangerous conditions

**Current Law - Child Labour (Prohibition and Regulation) Act:**
- Prohibition of employment of children below 14 years
- Regulation of working conditions where children work
- List of prohibited occupations and processes
- Penalties for violations

**Punishment:**
- Imprisonment: 3 months to 1 year
- Fine: Rs. 10,000 to 20,000
- Both imprisonment and fine

**Exceptions:**
Children can work:
- In family business/enterprises (after school hours)
- As child artists (with permission)
- In sports activities

**Related Constitutional Provisions:**
- Article 21A: Right to education (6-14 years)
- Article 39(e): Children not abused
- Article 39(f): Childhood protected
- Article 45: Early childhood care and education

## Article 25 - Freedom of Religion

**Article Number:** Article 25
**Part:** Part III - Fundamental Rights
**Type:** Right to Freedom of Religion

**Text:**
"Subject to public order, morality and health and to the other provisions of this Part, all persons are equally entitled to freedom of conscience and the right freely to profess, practice and propagate religion."

**Four Components:**

1. **Freedom of Conscience**
- Inner freedom to believe
- Personal faith
- Absolute right (no restrictions)

2. **Profess Religion**
- Declare one's religious beliefs
- External manifestation
- Subject to restrictions

3. **Practice Religion**
- Perform religious rites and rituals
- Religious observances
- Subject to restrictions

4. **Propagate Religion**
- Right to spread one's religion
- Right to explain and communicate
- Does NOT include right to convert by force, fraud, or allurement

**Restrictions on Religious Freedom:**
1. Public order
2. Morality
3. Health
4. Other fundamental rights
5. Social welfare and reform

**What State Can Do (Article 25(2)):**
- Regulate economic, financial, political, or secular activities associated with religion
- Provide for social welfare and reform
- Open Hindu religious institutions to all classes (Article 25(2)(b))

**Important Points:**
- Applies to all persons (citizens and non-citizens)
- Protection extends to religious minorities
- Essential religious practices protected
- Non-essential practices can be regulated

**Landmark Cases:**
- Commissioner, Hindu Religious Endowments v. Lakshmindra (1954) - Essential practices
- Mohd. Hanif Quareshi v. State of Bihar (1958) - Cow slaughter
- Bijoe Emmanuel v. State of Kerala (1986) - Jehovah's Witnesses case
- Indian Young Lawyers Association v. State of Kerala (2018) - Sabarimala case

## Article 26 - Freedom to Manage Religious Affairs

**Article Number:** Article 26
**Part:** Part III - Fundamental Rights
**Type:** Right to Freedom of Religion

**Text:**
"Subject to public order, morality and health, every religious denomination or any section thereof shall have the right to establish and maintain institutions for religious and charitable purposes, to manage its own affairs in matters of religion, to own and acquire movable and immovable property, and to administer such property in accordance with law."

**Four Rights of Religious Denominations:**

1. **Establish and Maintain Institutions**
- Religious institutions
- Charitable institutions
- Educational institutions

2. **Manage Own Affairs in Religious Matters**
- Autonomy in religious matters
- Appointment of priests
- Religious rituals and ceremonies
- Subject to state regulation in secular matters

3. **Own and Acquire Property**
- Movable property
- Immovable property
- Temples, churches, mosques, gurudwaras

4. **Administer Property**
- Manage religious property
- Subject to law
- State can regulate administration

**Conditions:**
- Subject to public order, morality, health
- Subject to other fundamental rights
- Secular activities can be regulated

**Religious Denomination Means:**
- Collection of individuals
- Common system of beliefs (doctrines)
- Common organization
- Designation by distinctive name

**Examples:**
- Sunni Muslims, Shia Muslims
- Roman Catholics, Protestants
- Shaivites, Vaishnavites

**Distinction from Article 25:**
- Article 25: Individual religious freedom
- Article 26: Collective religious freedom (denomination)

**Important Cases:**
- Durgah Committee v. Hussain Ali (1961)
- Sri Venkataramana Devaru v. State of Mysore (1958)
- N. Adithayan v. Travancore Devaswom Board (2002)

## Article 29 & 30 - Cultural and Educational Rights of Minorities

**Article Number:** Article 29 & 30
**Part:** Part III - Fundamental Rights
**Type:** Cultural and Educational Rights

**Article 29 - Protection of Interests of Minorities:**

**Article 29(1):**
"Any section of citizens residing in India having a distinct language, script or culture of its own shall have the right to conserve the same."

**Rights:**
- Conserve distinct language
- Conserve distinct script
- Conserve distinct culture
- Applies to all sections of citizens (not just minorities)

**Article 29(2):**
"No citizen shall be denied admission into any educational institution maintained by the State or receiving aid out of State funds on grounds only of religion, race, caste, language or any of them."

**Rights:**
- Non-discrimination in admission to state/aided institutions
- Protection against exclusion based on religion, race, caste, language

**Article 30 - Right of Minorities to Establish Educational Institutions:**

**Article 30(1):**
"All minorities, whether based on religion or language, shall have the right to establish and administer educational institutions of their choice."

**Rights of Minorities:**
1. Right to establish educational institutions
2. Right to administer such institutions
3. Protection from discrimination in grant of state aid
4. Protection from expropriation

**Who are Minorities:**
- Religious minorities (Muslims, Christians, Sikhs, Buddhists, Jains, Parsis)
- Linguistic minorities (determined state-wise)

**Article 30(1A) [Repealed]:**
- Related to compulsory acquisition of minority institutions
- Removed by 44th Amendment

**Article 30(2):**
State cannot discriminate in granting aid to minority educational institutions.

**Important Points:**
- Absolute right (except reasonable regulations)
- Cannot impose restrictions that destroy minority character
- Applies to citizens only (Article 29)
- Applies to minorities (Article 30)
- State can regulate standards, but not administration

**Landmark Cases:**
- T.M.A. Pai Foundation v. State of Karnataka (2002) - 11 Judge Bench
- Islamic Academy of Education v. State of Karnataka (2003)
- P.A. Inamdar v. State of Maharashtra (2005) - Unaided institutions

## Article 32 - Right to Constitutional Remedies

**Article Number:** Article 32
**Part:** Part III - Fundamental Rights
**Type:** Right to Constitutional Remedies

**Text:**
"The right to move the Supreme Court by appropriate proceedings for the enforcement of the rights conferred by this Part is guaranteed."

**Why Called "Heart and Soul" of Constitution:**
- Dr. B.R. Ambedkar called it heart and soul
- Without Article 32, fundamental rights would be meaningless
- Provides enforcement mechanism

**Supreme Court Powers (Article 32(2)):**
Supreme Court can issue five types of writs:

**1. Habeas Corpus ("To Have the Body")**
- Orders authority to produce detained person before court
- Tests legality of detention
- Used in cases of illegal detention, disappearance

**2. Mandamus ("We Command")**
- Orders public authority to perform duty
- Cannot issue against private individuals
- Used to compel performance of public duty

**3. Prohibition**
- Issued to inferior court/tribunal
- Prohibits from exceeding jurisdiction
- Preventive remedy (before decision)

**4. Certiorari**
- Issued to inferior court/tribunal
- Quashes order already made
- Curative remedy (after decision)

**5. Quo Warranto ("By What Authority")**
- Questions person's claim to public office
- Tests validity of appointment
- Prevents usurpation of public office

**Key Features:**
- Direct access to Supreme Court (no need to go through lower courts first)
- Supreme Court has wide discretion
- Can issue directions, orders, writs
- Cannot be suspended except during emergency (Article 359)

**Article 226 - High Court Powers:**
- Similar to Article 32
- High Courts can also issue writs
- Wider territorial jurisdiction than Supreme Court
- Can issue writs for other purposes also (not just fundamental rights)

**Difference between Article 32 and 226:**
- Article 32: Supreme Court, fundamental rights only
- Article 226: High Court, fundamental rights + other legal rights

**Important Cases:**
- A.K. Gopalan v. State of Madras (1950)
- Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala (1973)
- S.P. Gupta v. Union of India (1981) - Public Interest Litigation

## Important Constitutional Principles

**Basic Structure Doctrine:**
Following cannot be amended even by Parliament:
1. Supremacy of Constitution
2. Sovereign, democratic, republican nature
3. Secular character
4. Separation of powers
5. Federal character
6. Judicial review
7. Rule of law
8. Parliamentary system
9. Independence of judiciary
10. Limited power of Parliament to amend
11. Harmony and balance of Fundamental Rights

**Doctrine of Severability:**
- If part of law is unconstitutional, only that part struck down
- Remaining valid parts continue

**Doctrine of Eclipse:**
- Pre-constitutional laws inconsistent with fundamental rights are eclipsed (not dead)
- Revive if inconsistency removed

**Doctrine of Waiver:**
- Fundamental rights can be waived voluntarily
- Must be clear, unambiguous, and with full knowledge

**Reasonable Classification:**
- State can classify persons for different treatment
- Classification must be based on intelligible differentia
- Differentia must have rational relation to object

**Procedure Established by Law vs Due Process:**
- India adopted "procedure established by law" (Article 21)
- Now interpreted to include "due process" elements
- Procedure must be fair, just, and reasonable
