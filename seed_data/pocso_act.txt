=== PROTECTION OF CHILDREN FROM SEXUAL OFFENCES ACT, 2012 (POCSO) ===

**Act Number:** 32 of 2012
**Enacted:** May 22, 2012
**Effective Date:** November 14, 2012
**Amended:** 2019 (enhanced punishments)
**Total Sections:** 46 sections across 9 chapters
**Law Type:** Criminal Law - Child Protection

The Protection of Children from Sexual Offences (POCSO) Act, 2012 is a comprehensive legislation specifically enacted to protect children from sexual abuse, sexual harassment, and pornography. It is a gender-neutral law that defines a child as any person below 18 years of age and provides stringent punishments for various forms of sexual offences against children. The Act also mandates child-friendly procedures during investigation and trial to ensure the dignity and well-being of child victims.

**Key Features:**
- Gender-neutral: Protects all children regardless of gender
- Child-centric: Focuses on best interests of the child
- All offenses: Cognizable, non-bailable, non-compoundable
- Overrides IPC/BNS: POCSO takes precedence for child victims
- Mandatory reporting: Everyone must report child sexual abuse
- Special courts: Designated courts for speedy trial
- Child-friendly procedures: Protects child from re-traumatization

=== POCSO Section 2(1)(d): Child Defined ===

**Section Number:** Section 2(1)(d)
**Act Name:** Protection of Children from Sexual Offences Act, 2012
**Law Type:** Criminal Law - Child Protection
**Subject:** Definition of child

**Description:**
Section 2(1)(d) of the POCSO Act defines "child" to mean any person below the age of 18 years. This definition is crucial as it determines the applicability of the Act's protective provisions. The POCSO Act provides enhanced protection and stringent punishment specifically for offenses committed against persons below 18 years of age.

The age of 18 is aligned with international standards and India's Juvenile Justice Act. If the victim is below 18 years, POCSO Act applies mandatorily, and the accused cannot claim consent as a defense, as a child is legally incapable of consenting to sexual acts.

**Key Elements:**
1. Age threshold: Below 18 years (up to 17 years, 364 days)
2. Gender-neutral: Applies to boys and girls equally
3. No consent defense: Child cannot consent to sexual acts
4. Age determination: Medical examination if age uncertain
5. Victim protection: Identity protected throughout proceedings

**Application Examples:**
- 17-year-old girl sexually assaulted - POCSO applies (child victim)
- 16-year-old boy subjected to sexual harassment - POCSO applies (gender-neutral)
- 14-year-old in "consensual" relationship with 25-year-old - POCSO applies, consent irrelevant, 25-year-old liable
- Person claims victim is 18 but medical examination shows 17 - POCSO applies based on medical age determination
- 18-year-old victim - POCSO does not apply, BNS sexual offense provisions apply

**Age Determination:**
- If age disputed, court orders medical examination
- Medical opinion considered along with other evidence (birth certificate, school records)
- Benefit of doubt given to victim
- Ossification test, dental examination used for age estimation

**Related Sections:**
- POCSO Section 3-15: Various offenses against children
- BNS Section 63-70: Sexual offenses (apply if victim is 18 or above)
- Juvenile Justice Act: Child in conflict with law (also uses 18 years threshold)

**Important Notes:**
- POCSO Act takes precedence over BNS/IPC when victim is a child
- Mistake of age is not a defense if reasonable inquiry not made
- Even if child looks mature or claims to be 18, onus is on accused to verify
- Child pornography and related offenses also covered under this definition

=== POCSO Section 3-4: Penetrative Sexual Assault ===

**Section Number:** Section 3 (Offense) and Section 4 (Punishment)
**Act Name:** Protection of Children from Sexual Offences Act, 2012
**Law Type:** Criminal Law - Child Protection
**Offense:** Penetrative sexual assault on child

**Description:**
Section 3 defines penetrative sexual assault as the most serious form of sexual offense against a child. It includes any act of penetration into the vagina, urethra, anus, or mouth of a child by inserting any object or body part, or making the child do such acts. Section 4 prescribes rigorous imprisonment for not less than 10 years, which may extend to life imprisonment, along with a fine.

The offense is defined broadly to cover all forms of penetrative acts, recognizing that children need maximum protection from such invasive violations. The minimum sentence of 10 years (increased from 7 years in 2019 amendment) reflects the gravity of the offense.

**Acts Constituting Penetrative Sexual Assault (Section 3):**

1. Penetrating penis, or any object, or any body part into vagina, mouth, urethra, or anus of a child
2. Making the child penetrate any person's vagina, urethra, anus, or mouth
3. Applying mouth to penis, vagina, anus, urethra of a child
4. Making the child apply mouth to person's penis, vagina, anus, urethra
5. Any act of penetration, however slight, constitutes the offense

**Punishment (Section 4):**
- Minimum: Rigorous imprisonment for 10 years
- Maximum: Life imprisonment (meaning imprisonment for remainder of natural life)
- Fine: In addition to imprisonment
- Amendment 2019: Minimum sentence increased from 7 to 10 years

**Key Elements:**
1. Victim must be a child (below 18 years)
2. Act of penetration (however slight) must occur
3. Consent is irrelevant - child cannot consent
4. Gender-neutral: Protects boys and girls
5. Includes making child penetrate another person

**Practical Examples:**
- 25-year-old man has sexual intercourse with 15-year-old girl even with her "consent" - Penetrative sexual assault, minimum 10 years RI
- Teacher inserts finger into private parts of 8-year-old student - Penetrative sexual assault, 10 years to life
- Adult forces 12-year-old boy to perform oral sex - Penetrative sexual assault, minimum 10 years
- 17-year-old girl forced to insert object into another person's body - Victim of penetrative sexual assault
- Step-father repeatedly rapes 14-year-old daughter - Aggravated penetrative sexual assault (Section 5-6)

**Related Sections:**
- POCSO Section 5-6: Aggravated penetrative sexual assault (enhanced punishment)
- POCSO Section 42: Presumption of culpable mental state
- BNS Section 63: Rape (applies to victims 18 years and above)
- BSA Section 23: Confession to police (inadmissible)

**Bail Status:** Non-bailable
**Cognizable:** Yes (police can arrest without warrant)
**Compoundable:** No (cannot be settled between parties)
**Triable By:** Special Court designated under POCSO

**Important Notes:**
- Minimum sentence of 10 years cannot be reduced by court
- Life imprisonment means imprisonment for remainder of natural life, not 14/20 years
- Victim's testimony is sufficient for conviction, corroboration not mandatory (Section 33(2))
- Medical evidence strengthens case but not essential
- Special procedures ensure child-friendly trial
- Victim's identity must be protected, cannot be disclosed

=== POCSO Section 5-6: Aggravated Penetrative Sexual Assault ===

**Section Number:** Section 5 (Offense) and Section 6 (Punishment)
**Act Name:** Protection of Children from Sexual Offences Act, 2012
**Law Type:** Criminal Law - Child Protection
**Offense:** Aggravated penetrative sexual assault on child

**Description:**
Section 5 defines aggravated penetrative sexual assault, which covers penetrative sexual assault committed in aggravating circumstances that make the offense more heinous. Section 6 prescribes rigorous imprisonment for not less than 20 years, which may extend to life imprisonment (meaning remainder of natural life), along with a fine. In cases where the victim is below 16 years, death penalty may also be awarded (added in 2019 amendment).

The enhanced punishment reflects society's condemnation of sexual abuse by persons in positions of trust, armed forces personnel, public servants, or involving extreme violence, repeated acts, or particularly vulnerable children.

**Aggravating Circumstances (Section 5):**

**1. Perpetrator's Position of Authority:**
- Police officer, within their jurisdiction or duty limits
- Member of armed forces or security forces
- Public servant
- Jail, hospital, or remand home staff
- Management or staff of educational institution
- Religious institution management or staff

**2. Relationship with Child:**
- Relative of the child
- Person living in shared household with child
- Person in position of trust or authority (teacher, doctor, guardian)

**3. Circumstances of Offense:**
- Committed on pregnant child
- Inflicts grievous hurt, bodily harm, injury, or danger to life
- Repeated offense on same child
- Victim is below 12 years (especially heinous)
- Victim is mentally ill, intellectually disabled, or physically disabled
- Uses deadly weapon or fire/corrosive substance
- Gang penetrative sexual assault (shared intention by group)

**4. Nature of Act:**
- Causes child to become pregnant
- Transmits life-threatening disease
- Takes advantage of child's mental or physical disability
- Commits during communal violence, sectarian riots, or natural calamity

**Punishment (Section 6):**
- Minimum: Rigorous imprisonment for 20 years (death for victim below 16 - added 2019)
- Maximum: Life imprisonment (remainder of natural life) or death
- Fine: In addition to imprisonment
- Death penalty: Court's discretion for victims below 16 years

**Practical Examples:**
- School principal sexually assaults 10-year-old student in school premises - Aggravated (position of authority), minimum 20 years or death
- Father repeatedly rapes 14-year-old daughter - Aggravated (relative, repeated), minimum 20 years or death
- Doctor in government hospital sexually assaults child patient - Aggravated (public servant, trust), minimum 20 years
- Gang of men assault 7-year-old girl causing grievous injuries - Aggravated (below 12 years, gang, injury), death penalty possible
- Person sexually assaults 13-year-old with mental disability - Aggravated (disability), minimum 20 years or death
- Armed forces personnel assaults child during conflict - Aggravated (armed forces, position), minimum 20 years

**Related Sections:**
- POCSO Section 3-4: Penetrative sexual assault (non-aggravated)
- POCSO Section 42: Presumption of culpable mental state
- BNS Section 64: Aggravated forms of rape
- BNS Section 103(1): Punishment of death or life imprisonment for murder

**Bail Status:** Non-bailable
**Cognizable:** Yes
**Compoundable:** No
**Triable By:** Special Court

**Important Notes:**
- 2019 amendment introduced death penalty for assault on children below 16 years
- Death penalty is discretionary, not mandatory, even for victims below 16
- Courts must consider rehabilitation of offender alongside gravity of offense
- Life imprisonment means remainder of natural life, not 14/20 years
- Multiple aggravating circumstances can exist in single case
- Burden on prosecution to prove aggravating circumstances beyond reasonable doubt

=== POCSO Section 7-9: Sexual Assault and Aggravated Sexual Assault ===

**Section Number:** Section 7 (Offense), Section 8 (Punishment), Section 9 (Aggravated)
**Act Name:** Protection of Children from Sexual Offences Act, 2012
**Law Type:** Criminal Law - Child Protection
**Offense:** Sexual assault without penetration

**Description:**
Sections 7-9 deal with sexual assault that does not involve penetration. Section 7 defines sexual assault as touching the private parts of a child with sexual intent, or making the child touch the private parts of the accused or any other person. Section 8 prescribes imprisonment for a term which shall not be less than 3 years but may extend to 5 years, and fine. Section 9 covers aggravated sexual assault with enhanced punishment of 5 to 7 years imprisonment.

This recognizes that sexual abuse without penetration is also deeply traumatic for children and must be punished stringently.

**Acts Constituting Sexual Assault (Section 7):**

1. Touching vagina, penis, anus, or breasts of child with sexual intent
2. Making child touch vagina, penis, anus, or breasts of accused or any other person
3. Doing any other act with sexual intent involving physical contact without penetration

**Punishment for Sexual Assault (Section 8):**
- Minimum: Imprisonment for 3 years
- Maximum: Imprisonment for 5 years
- Fine: In addition to imprisonment

**Aggravated Sexual Assault (Section 9):**
Same aggravating circumstances as in Section 5 apply (police officer, armed forces, public servant, relative, person in authority, child below 12, disabled child, repeated offense, etc.)

**Punishment for Aggravated Sexual Assault (Section 10):**
- Minimum: Imprisonment for 5 years
- Maximum: Imprisonment for 7 years
- Fine: In addition to imprisonment

**Practical Examples:**
- Teacher touches private parts of 12-year-old student - Sexual assault, 3-5 years
- Uncle inappropriately touches 9-year-old niece's breasts - Aggravated sexual assault (relative, below 12), 5-7 years
- Stranger grabs 15-year-old's private parts in public transport - Sexual assault, 3-5 years
- Coach makes 10-year-old athlete touch coach's private parts - Sexual assault, 3-5 years
- Doctor inappropriately touches child during examination with sexual intent - Aggravated sexual assault (public servant), 5-7 years

**Related Sections:**
- POCSO Section 3-6: Penetrative sexual assault (more serious offense)
- POCSO Section 11-12: Sexual harassment (less serious, no touching of private parts)
- BNS Section 74: Assault or use of criminal force to woman with intent to outrage modesty

**Bail Status:** Non-bailable
**Cognizable:** Yes
**Compoundable:** No

**Important Notes:**
- Sexual intent must be proved for conviction
- Touching of private parts differentiates sexual assault from sexual harassment
- Aggravating circumstances increase punishment by 2 years (5-7 instead of 3-5)
- Medical examination may be conducted but not mandatory
- Child's statement is primary evidence

=== POCSO Section 11-12: Sexual Harassment ===

**Section Number:** Section 11 (Offense) and Section 12 (Punishment)
**Act Name:** Protection of Children from Sexual Offences Act, 2012
**Law Type:** Criminal Law - Child Protection
**Offense:** Sexual harassment of child

**Description:**
Section 11 defines sexual harassment as any person who, with sexual intent, utters words, makes sounds or gestures, exhibits objects or body parts, makes a child exhibit their body, or follows or contacts a child repeatedly. Section 12 prescribes imprisonment for a term which may extend to 3 years, and fine.

Sexual harassment covers a broad spectrum of non-contact sexual offenses that traumatize and violate the dignity of children. It recognizes that harm can be caused even without physical touch.

**Acts Constituting Sexual Harassment (Section 11):**

1. Uttering words, making sounds, or gestures with sexual intent
2. Exhibiting any object with sexual intent (pornography, private parts)
3. Making a child exhibit their body or body parts
4. Repeatedly following or stalking a child
5. Threatening to use real or fabricated sexual images of child
6. Enticing child for pornographic purposes

**Punishment (Section 12):**
- Imprisonment: Up to 3 years
- Fine: In addition to imprisonment

**Practical Examples:**
- Person makes sexual comments to 14-year-old girl on street - Sexual harassment, up to 3 years
- Man follows 16-year-old child from school repeatedly - Sexual harassment (stalking), up to 3 years
- Person shows pornographic images to 12-year-old child - Sexual harassment, up to 3 years
- Teacher makes sexual gestures toward student - Sexual harassment, up to 3 years
- Person threatens to circulate morphed sexual images of child - Sexual harassment, up to 3 years
- Adult repeatedly contacts child via social media with sexual messages - Sexual harassment (repeated contact), up to 3 years

**Key Elements:**
1. Sexual intent must be present
2. No physical touch required
3. Includes verbal, gestural, and visual harassment
4. Repeated contact or following constitutes harassment
5. Victim's age below 18 years

**Related Sections:**
- POCSO Section 7-10: Sexual assault (involves touching private parts)
- POCSO Section 13-14: Use of child for pornographic purposes
- BNS Section 79: Word, gesture, or act intended to insult modesty of woman
- IT Act Section 67: Publishing obscene material

**Bail Status:** Bailable (punishment up to 3 years)
**Cognizable:** Yes
**Compoundable:** No

**Important Notes:**
- Sexual harassment does not require physical contact
- Cyberstalking and online harassment covered under this section
- Sexual intent may be inferred from circumstances
- Child's testimony about harassment is sufficient
- Even single instance of showing pornography or making sexual comments constitutes offense

=== POCSO Section 15: Storage of Pornographic Material Involving Child ===

**Section Number:** Section 15
**Act Name:** Protection of Children from Sexual Offences Act, 2012
**Law Type:** Criminal Law - Child Protection
**Offense:** Storing or possessing child pornography

**Description:**
Section 15 makes it an offense for any person to store or possess pornographic material in any form involving a child for commercial purposes, except for use as evidence in court or for the purpose of investigation. This section targets the demand side of child pornography by criminalizing possession, thereby disrupting the child sexual abuse material (CSAM) market.

The offense recognizes that child pornography perpetuates abuse, re-victimizes children, and fuels demand for new abuse material.

**Key Elements:**

1. **Act:** Storing or possessing child pornographic material
2. **Form:** Any form - digital, physical, videos, images, illustrations
3. **Commercial Purpose:** For distribution, sale, transmission, or commercial use
4. **Exceptions:** Possession for use in court as evidence or for investigation

**Punishment:**
- First offense: Imprisonment up to 3 years or fine, or both
- Subsequent offense: Imprisonment up to 5 years and fine

**What Constitutes Child Pornography:**
- Any visual depiction of sexually explicit conduct involving a child
- Includes photographs, videos, digital images, computer-generated images
- Includes depiction of child's genitals, pubic area, breasts (for female child)
- Covers realistic depiction that appears to be child

**Practical Examples:**
- Person stores child sexual abuse videos on laptop for sharing on dark web - Offense under Section 15, up to 3 years
- Individual downloads child pornography for personal viewing (no commercial purpose) - May be covered under IT Act Section 67B
- Police officer stores evidence of child pornography for investigation - Exception, not an offense
- Person stores child pornography to sell to others - Section 15 offense, up to 3 years first offense
- Second offense of storing child pornography for commercial purposes - Up to 5 years and fine

**Related Sections:**
- POCSO Section 13-14: Using child for pornographic purposes (more serious, 5-7 years)
- IT Act Section 67B: Publishing or transmitting child sexual abuse material
- POCSO Section 16-17: Abetment and attempt

**Cognizable:** Yes
**Bail Status:** Bailable (punishment up to 3 years first offense)
**Compoundable:** No

**Important Notes:**
- "Commercial purpose" is broadly interpreted to include sharing, distribution, or exchange
- Even possession without commercial purpose may be prosecuted under IT Act Section 67B
- Digital forensics used to establish storage and access of child pornography
- Burden on accused to prove possession was for legitimate purpose (evidence/investigation)
- Internet service providers and intermediaries have duty to report child pornography

=== POCSO Section 19: Mandatory Reporting of Sexual Offenses ===

**Section Number:** Section 19
**Act Name:** Protection of Children from Sexual Offences Act, 2012
**Law Type:** Criminal Law - Child Protection
**Subject:** Duty to report child sexual abuse

**Description:**
Section 19 imposes a mandatory legal duty on every person who has knowledge or apprehension that a child sexual offense has been committed, or is likely to be committed, to immediately report the information to the Special Juvenile Police Unit (SJPU) or local police. This applies to all persons including parents, teachers, doctors, neighbors, and professionals.

Mandatory reporting is crucial for early intervention, preventing further abuse, and ensuring that child victims receive protection and support. Failure to report is a punishable offense under Section 21.

**Mandatory Reporting Requirements:**

**Who Must Report (Section 19(1)):**
- Every person including:
  - Teachers and school staff
  - Doctors, nurses, and medical professionals
  - Parents and family members
  - NGO workers and social workers
  - Media professionals
  - Hotel and lodge owners/staff
  - Neighbors and community members

**What to Report:**
- Any information or knowledge that a child sexual offense has been committed
- Apprehension or suspicion that such offense is likely to be committed
- Any attempt to commit such offense

**When to Report:**
- Within 24 hours of gaining knowledge or apprehension
- Immediately upon witnessing the offense

**Where to Report:**
- Special Juvenile Police Unit (SJPU), or
- Local police station

**Punishment for Failure to Report (Section 21):**
- Imprisonment: Up to 6 months, or
- Fine, or
- Both

**Practical Examples:**
- Teacher notices signs of sexual abuse on student (bruises, fear, behavioral changes) - Must report within 24 hours, failure punishable
- Doctor examining child finds evidence of sexual assault - Must report immediately, failure is offense
- Neighbor hears child screaming and suspects sexual abuse - Must report to police within 24 hours
- Hotel staff sees adult taking underage child to room in suspicious circumstances - Must report immediately
- Parent discovers stepfather has been sexually abusing child - Must report within 24 hours despite family relationship
- Social worker learns of ongoing child sexual abuse during counseling - Must report immediately

**Exceptions:**
- Psychiatrists, psychologists, social workers, or counselors reporting in the course of treatment are covered under separate provisions for ensuring child's safety while maintaining therapeutic relationship

**Who is Exempted:**
- The child victim themselves is not required to report (no punishment if child does not report own abuse)

**Related Sections:**
- POCSO Section 21: Punishment for failure to report or record
- POCSO Section 20: Procedure for reporting
- POCSO Section 24: Recording of statement of child
- JJ Act Section 109: Punishment for cruelty to child

**Important Notes:**
- Mandatory reporting overrides confidentiality in most professional relationships
- Medical professionals must file medico-legal case in all cases of child sexual abuse
- Even suspicion or apprehension triggers duty to report, certainty not required
- Failure to report makes the person complicit in allowing abuse to continue
- Immunity from civil/criminal liability for good faith reporting
- Reporting can be done by phone, written complaint, or in person
- Police must register FIR immediately upon receiving report, no preliminary inquiry

=== POCSO Section 28-29: Special Courts and Presumptions ===

**Section Number:** Section 28 (Special Courts) and Section 29 (Presumptions)
**Act Name:** Protection of Children from Sexual Offences Act, 2012
**Law Type:** Criminal Law - Child Protection
**Subject:** Designated courts and legal presumptions

**Description:**
Section 28 mandates the establishment of Special Courts in each district to try POCSO offenses. Section 29 creates legal presumptions that aid prosecution by shifting certain burdens of proof to the accused. These provisions ensure speedy, specialized, and victim-friendly adjudication of child sexual abuse cases.

**Special Courts (Section 28):**

**Establishment:**
- State Government, in consultation with Chief Justice of High Court, designates a Sessions Court as Special Court for each district
- May designate more than one Special Court depending on caseload
- Existing Sessions Court judge may be designated to handle POCSO cases

**Composition:**
- Presided over by Sessions Judge
- Special Public Prosecutor appointed for POCSO cases
- Support persons and interpreters available

**Purpose:**
- Ensure speedy trial and disposal of cases
- Provide child-friendly environment
- Specialized handling of sensitive cases
- Reduce backlog and delays

**Timeline:**
- Trial to be completed preferably within one year from date of taking cognizance
- Court may extend for recorded reasons if necessary

**Presumptions (Section 29):**

**Presumption of Culpable Mental State:**
Section 29 provides that in prosecution for any offense under the POCSO Act which requires culpable mental state on the part of the accused, the Special Court shall presume the existence of such mental state. The burden shifts to the accused to prove that they did not have the requisite culpable mental state.

**What This Means:**
- Prosecution does not need to prove accused had sexual intent or knowledge
- Court presumes accused had requisite intent if act is proved
- Accused must prove they did not have such intent (difficult to discharge)
- Significantly eases prosecution's burden of proof

**Practical Examples:**
- Accused touched child's private parts - Court presumes sexual intent, accused must prove touching was accidental or for medical reasons
- Accused showed pornography to child - Court presumes sexual intent, accused must prove legitimate purpose
- Accused repeatedly followed child - Court presumes sexual intent, accused must prove innocent coincidence

**Related Sections:**
- POCSO Section 33: Procedure and powers of Special Court
- POCSO Section 35: Guidelines for trial
- POCSO Section 42: Offenses triable by Special Court only
- BNSS: Criminal procedure provisions applicable

**Important Notes:**
- Special Courts have same powers as Sessions Court
- Presumption under Section 29 is rebuttable but difficult to overcome
- Special Court can take cognizance of offense without committal proceedings
- Special Courts should have child-friendly facilities (waiting areas, no robes, simple language)

=== POCSO Section 33: Child-Friendly Procedures and Special Protections ===

**Section Number:** Section 33
**Act Name:** Protection of Children from Sexual Offences Act, 2012
**Law Type:** Criminal Law - Child Protection
**Subject:** Procedure and powers of Special Court to ensure child-friendly trial

**Description:**
Section 33 is one of the most important provisions of the POCSO Act, establishing comprehensive child-friendly procedures to protect child victims from re-traumatization during trial. It empowers Special Courts to create a safe, supportive, and non-intimidating environment for children to testify. The section recognizes that the trial process itself can be traumatic for children and mandates specific protections.

**Child-Friendly Procedures:**

**1. Testimony Recording (Section 33(1)):**
- Child's testimony recorded in camera (closed court, no public access)
- Only child, family members/guardians, Special Public Prosecutor, support person, and accused's lawyer present
- Accused may be screened or removed if child is uncomfortable, but can observe via video link
- Frequent breaks during testimony to prevent fatigue

**2. Support Person (Section 33(4)):**
- Child entitled to support person of choice during testimony
- Support person can be parent, guardian, social worker, or trusted adult
- Support person provides emotional support but does not prompt answers

**3. Questioning (Section 33(2)):**
- Court may disallow aggressive or character-assassination questions
- Leading questions that confuse or intimidate child not permitted
- Cross-examination conducted sensitively
- Simple language used, no legal jargon

**4. Recording Methods (Section 33(5)):**
- Testimony may be video-recorded for use in other proceedings
- Reduces need for child to testify multiple times
- Video recording becomes part of case record

**5. Identity Protection (Section 33(7)):**
- Identity of child must not be disclosed
- Media prohibited from publishing name, address, photograph, or any information revealing child's identity
- Violation punishable under Section 23 (6 months to 1 year imprisonment)

**6. Compensation (Section 33(8)):**
- Court may order interim and final compensation to child victim
- Compensation for physical harm, mental trauma, rehabilitation
- Paid from government fund or from convicted person

**7. Medical Examination (Section 27):**
- Conducted by female doctor or in presence of woman
- Conducted in hospital setting with parent/guardian present
- Medical report submitted within 5 days

**Evidentiary Provisions:**

**1. Testimony Sufficient (Section 33(2)):**
- Child's testimony is sufficient for conviction
- Corroboration not mandatory
- Court may convict based solely on child's credible testimony

**2. Questions About Character Not Allowed:**
- Child's character or past sexual experience cannot be brought up
- Prevents victim-blaming and character assassination

**Practical Examples:**
- 9-year-old testifies in closed court with mother as support person, accused observes via video link - Proper procedure
- Defense lawyer tries to ask child about clothing worn at time of assault (implying provocation) - Court disallows as character assassination
- Child breaks down during testimony - Court orders break and postpones further questioning to next day
- Newspaper publishes name and photo of child victim - Offense under Section 23, editor liable for imprisonment
- Court awards â‚¹5 lakhs compensation to child victim for medical treatment and counseling - Proper exercise of power under Section 33(8)

**Related Sections:**
- POCSO Section 24: Recording of statement by Magistrate
- POCSO Section 27: Medical examination
- POCSO Section 23: Disclosure of identity of child (punishment)
- POCSO Section 39: Compensation by State Government

**Important Notes:**
- POCSO trial procedures prioritize child's psychological well-being
- Courts must be sensitized to trauma of child sexual abuse
- Child should not see accused during testimony if it causes distress
- Testimony should be recorded in child's language or mother tongue
- Support person should not coach or prompt child, only provide comfort
- Special Courts should have separate waiting areas, toys, child-friendly furniture
- Judges and lawyers should not wear robes to avoid intimidating child
- Trial should be completed within one year to prevent prolonged trauma

=== POCSO Act: General Provisions and Key Principles ===

**Subject:** Overarching principles and provisions

**Description:**
The POCSO Act establishes a comprehensive legal framework for protection of children from sexual offenses with several overriding principles and general provisions that apply throughout the Act.

**Overriding Effect:**

**POCSO Prevails Over IPC/BNS (Section 42A):**
- If same offense covered under POCSO and BNS/IPC, POCSO provisions shall apply
- POCSO punishments generally higher than corresponding BNS offenses
- Example: Rape of 17-year-old - POCSO Section 3-4 applies (10 years to life), not BNS Section 63

**All Offenses: Cognizable, Non-Bailable, Non-Compoundable:**
- Police can arrest without warrant
- Bail very difficult to obtain, generally not granted except in exceptional circumstances
- Parties cannot settle or compromise the case

**Gender Neutral:**
- Both boys and girls protected equally
- Perpetrator can be of any gender
- Recognizes that boys are also victims of sexual abuse

**Key Procedural Safeguards:**

**1. Medical Examination (Section 27):**
- Conducted by female doctor or in presence of woman
- In hospital setting with parent/guardian
- Report submitted within 5 days

**2. Statement Recording (Section 24):**
- Recorded by Magistrate at residence of child or convenient place
- Recording by female officer if child is female
- Officer not in uniform
- Video recording of statement

**3. Investigation (Section 173, BNSS as applicable):**
- Investigation to be completed within 3-6 months
- Special Juvenile Police Unit (SJPU) conducts investigation
- SJPU officers specially trained in child-friendly investigation

**4. Trial (Sections 28-35):**
- Trial in camera (closed court)
- Trial to be completed preferably within one year
- Child-friendly environment
- Minimal contact with accused

**Child Welfare Measures:**

**1. Interim Compensation:**
- Available for immediate relief and medical treatment
- Child Welfare Committee may recommend
- State Government pays from victim compensation fund

**2. Rehabilitation:**
- State responsible for care and protection of child victim
- Medical treatment, counseling, and therapy
- Educational and vocational support
- Shelter if needed

**3. Confidentiality:**
- Identity protected at all stages
- Media prohibited from publishing identifying information
- Violation is punishable offense

**Related Offenses:**

- Abetment (Section 16): Punishable same as main offense
- Attempt (Section 17): Punishable with up to half of maximum punishment
- Failure to report (Section 21): Up to 6 months imprisonment
- False complaint (Section 22): Up to 6 months imprisonment or fine if person makes false complaint or provides false information knowing it to be false

**Important Notes:**
- POCSO is victim-centric legislation focused on child's best interests
- Applies to all children regardless of gender, religion, caste, class
- Consent is irrelevant - child cannot consent to sexual acts
- Speedy trial and victim support are core principles
- Act must be read harmoniously with JJ Act, BNSS, and other child protection laws
- State has positive duty to protect and rehabilitate child victims
